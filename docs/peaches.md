### Likely Contributing Factors (in order of suspicion)
1. **Environmental allergies causing BOTH skin and GI symptoms** — Ekici & Ok 2024 (PMC11034634) found 77% of dogs with atopic dermatitis had diarrhea, with measurable intestinal epithelial damage (elevated TFF-3 and IAP biomarkers = "leaky gut"). Most common allergen in that study: mould mites (16/26 dogs). Environmental allergens trigger systemic immune response → gut lining inflammation → diarrhea. This is not rare — it's the majority presentation. Snowmelt releases mold spores + mould mites become active. Clinician's Brief confirms CAD "can be associated with other systemic signs (eg, GI, respiratory)." Indoor allergens (dust mites, indoor mold) are also relevant — not just outdoor.
2. **Zenrelia GI side effects** — may be cumulative over months, or worsening as spring allergen load increases and the drug works harder. The 4.25mg dose controlled itching in winter but is now breaking through.
3. **Portion increase** (215g -> 235g per meal) — 10% more food = more fat, may exceed digestive capacity in a GI-sensitive dog. Dose-dependent intolerance.
4. **Combination of all three** — each factor alone might be tolerable, but stacked together they overwhelm his gut.

### Ruled Out
- Treats — removed for 4 days with no improvement, were hydrolyzed anyway
- Pumpkin topper — has always been part of his diet, helps stool when present

## Medication Comparison Notes
- **Zenrelia** (JAK1/JAK2/TYK2 inhibitor): oral daily pill, blocks itch + inflammation broadly, #1 side effect is GI (diarrhea/vomiting), immunosuppressive
- **Cytopoint** (monoclonal antibody targeting IL-31): injection every 4-8 weeks, blocks itch signal only, GI safety comparable to saline placebo, not reversible once injected (stays 4-8 weeks), can lose efficacy over time (anti-drug antibodies), 87% maintained benefit at 12 months in long-term study
- **Apoquel** (JAK1 inhibitor): same drug class as Zenrelia, likely similar GI issues, 45% of dogs flare when stepping down dose
- **Cyclosporine** (Atopica): worse GI side effects than JAK inhibitors, slower onset (4-6 weeks)
